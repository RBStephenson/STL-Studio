---
name: White and Black Rule
description: Pure white reserved for final specular highlights only; pure black reserved for deepest occlusion, ink lining, pupil dots, and canonically black materials. Default shadow anchor is Payne's Grey (Expert Acrylics). Default working highlight is a tinted near-white warm or cool to match light source temperature.
type: reference
---
Never use pure white or pure black as general-purpose painting colors.

**Why:** These extremes flatten surfaces, kill temperature, and make paint jobs look unfinished rather than refined. They have no temperature and integrate poorly into transitions.

**How to apply:**

**Whites — Highlight Hierarchy**
- Working highlight (broad passes): tinted near-white matched to light source temperature.
  - Warm light → Bright Ivory, Heavy Warm White S18, or pale cream mix
  - Cool/neutral light → heavily thinned pale blue-grey (Payne's Grey + Titanium White high ratio) or cool off-white
- Final specular highlight (single dot or thin edge only): Pure Titanium White or Carbon White — hottest point of direct light contact only. One small dot.
- Exception: canonically flat white materials (white fabric, white armor panels) use near-white mid-tone with pure white specular — same hierarchy, compressed range.

**Blacks — Shadow Hierarchy**
- Shadow base / zenithal shadow anchor: **Payne's Grey — MEA-013 (Expert Acrylics)** — default working "black." Cool blue-grey temperature gives depth and integrates with color layers. Use wherever you'd reflexively reach for black in a shadow.
- Deepest occlusion recesses (contact points, deep undercuts): Carbon Black (Pro Acryl) or Coal Black 002 — only where light genuinely cannot reach. Accent points, not base layers.
- Pure black — permitted uses only:
  - Ink lining and panel line separation
  - Pupil dots and eye detail
  - Canonically flat black materials (rubber, certain plastics, black leather with no sheen)
  - Priming (Primer Black as zenithal base — not a painting color)

**Decision Tree (before reaching for white or black):**
1. Final specular dot or absolute deepest recess? → White or Carbon Black permitted.
2. Canonically that color as a material? → Permitted, specular hierarchy still applies on top.
3. Just trying to lighten or darken? → Stop. Use tinted near-white or Payne's Grey instead.
