## Skin Tab Structure

For every figure with paintable skin, **select the single most appropriate method** using the decision table below, and present **only that method** in the Skin tab. Lead with a brief character analysis justifying the choice, then give the step-by-step for the chosen method. Do not list all three — the other methods exist here as a reference for choosing, not to reproduce in every guide.

**The mid-tone is the anchor for all three methods** — it represents the true skin color. Always identify the mid-tone first, mapped to actual Pro Acryl or Fanatic codes.

## Method 1 — Basic / Default (Primer → Undercoat → Base → Mid → Highlight)

**Concept:** Classic layering. Works for all skin types and scales. Most controllable method.

**Sequence:**
1. Prime (grey or flesh-toned primer — avoid white for warm skin, avoid black for fair complexions unless using zenithal)
2. Undercoat with a slightly darkened version of the base tone (adds automatic shadow)
3. Base tone — paint the full skin area
4. Mid-tone — layer leaving shadow recesses showing through
5. Highlight — thin line/edge highlight on raised areas (cheekbones, nose bridge, brow ridge, chin, knuckles)
6. Optional: thin glaze of Transparent Yellow 049 to unify warmth; or Transparent Red 047 / FW Crimson Ink undiluted on cheeks/nose and wiped back for flush

**Pro Acryl recipe example (fair skin):**
- Shadow (deep recess): Burnt Umber 018 + Coal Black 002 at 2:1
- Base/shadow: Shadow Flesh 042 (~35% value)
- Mid-tone: Warm Flesh 073 (~55% value)
- Highlight 1: Warm Flesh 073 + Bright Shadow Flesh S41 at 1:1
- Highlight 2: Bright Shadow Flesh S41 or Heavy Warm White S18 for final specular (~90% value)
- Optional flush accent: Transparent Red 047 or FW Crimson Ink undiluted (apply sparingly and wipe back) over cheeks/nose — do not thin, both shift magenta at heavy dilution. See [[transparent-red-substitution]]

**Pros:** Most predictable. Easy to correct. Works at any scale.
**Cons:** Can look flat without enough layers.
**Best for:** First-time painters, tabletop (28mm), speed priority, any skin tone, cartoon/anime (2–3 layers max, no freckling).

## Method 2A — The Pinkle Method (Fair / Feminine / Heroic)

**Concept:** Magenta underpainting mimics sub-surface scattering from beneath thin flesh layers.

⚠️ **DO NOT use Flesh Wash (MPA-202) for the pinkle step.** Flesh Wash is dark and opaque — it will not create SSS. Flesh Wash should never appear in Method 2A or 2B.

**Canonical pinkle paint (step 3):**
- **MPA-011 Magenta** (#BB4D73) thinned 1:4 — the pinkle. No wash substitutes.

**Sequence:**
1. Black primer
2. White zenithal (heavy from top, fades to nothing at bottom)
3. Magenta all over (thin, transparent — the "pinkle"): MPA-011 Magenta thinned 1:4
4. Transparent Red 047 or FW Crimson Ink undiluted into recesses, shadows, and bendy parts (knuckles, elbow creases, eyelids, under chin) — do not thin. See [[transparent-red-substitution]]
5. Thin base tone layer over the zenithal — leave shadows untouched: Warm Flesh 073 or Shadow Flesh 042
6. Thin mid-tone layer — leave deepest shadows untouched
7. Very diluted highlight — almost a glaze catching uppermost raised surfaces: Bright Shadow Flesh S41 or mix with Heavy Warm White S18

**Pros:** Stunning sub-surface scattering. Luminous, fewer layers. Shadows read naturally warm and deep.
**Cons:** Requires good zenithal — poor zenithal = muddy result. Magenta can overpower if too thick. Harder to correct.
**Best for:** Female figures, fair-skinned characters, protagonist/hero figures, display 1:6 / 75mm / busts.

## Method 2B — Red Zenithal (Rugged / Masculine / Weathered)

**Same as 2A but skip the magenta.** Go straight to **FW Crimson Ink thinned 1:2** over the whole figure after the white zenithal. Then follow steps 5–7.

⚠️ **For this diluted whole-figure filter step, use FW Crimson Ink (513) at 1:2** — PA Transparent Red shifts magenta at this dilution level. (Undiluted PA Transparent Red is fine for localized accent work; see [[transparent-red-substitution]].)
⚠️ **DO NOT use Flesh Wash (MPA-202) here.** Too dark and opaque — muddles the zenithal.

**Best for:** Rugged male, military, monster, or weathered figures. Works for darker complexions where magenta would over-pink.

## Method 3 — Wash Tinting (Mid-tone → Varnish → Yellow / Blue / Red Wash Layers)

**Concept:** From fine art figure painting. Mid-tone is the "true" skin color, then transparent washes of the three primaries are selectively applied and wiped to tint zones with realistic undertones.

**Sequence:**
1. Prime (grey or white — avoid black)
2. Apply the mid-tone evenly as the full base — this IS the skin color
3. Seal with satin varnish (CRITICAL — each wash layer must be isolated)
4. **Yellow wash** (1:10 minimum): fatty areas — cheeks, forehead, belly, backs of hands. Use Transparent Yellow 049 thinned 1:8 or Warm Yellow 072 as very thin glaze. Remove excess immediately. Dry fully.
5. Seal with satin varnish
6. **Blue wash** (thin): vein areas — temples, wrists, eyelids, beard zone, under eyes. Use Transparent Blue 046 thinned 1:8 or Payne's Grey S02 as glaze. Wipe back. Dry fully.
7. Seal with satin varnish
8. **Red accent** (capillary zones — nose tip, ear rims, knuckles, lips, cheeks): Use Transparent Red 047 or FW Crimson Ink undiluted. Apply with a fine brush and wipe back immediately — do not pre-thin (both shift magenta at 1:4+ dilution). Dry fully. See [[transparent-red-substitution]].
9. Final seal. Add highlights if desired over the top.

**Speedpaint 2.0 filter variant:** After first seal, apply Peachy Flesh Speedpaint 2.0 thinned 1:3 (Speedpaint:Speedpaint Medium) as a warm filter — alternative to the yellow wash. Can be combined with subsequent Pro Acryl Transparent Blue and Red washes. **Always use Speedpaint Medium, not water, to thin Speedpaint 2.0.**

**Pros:** Most realistic tones achievable. Each undertone zone is precisely mapped. Professional results.
**Cons:** Slowest method — 3 varnish layers and dry times. One wash too thick = muddy result. Not suitable for 28mm (too subtle at small scale).
**Best for:** Display 1:6, 75mm, busts. Realistic military/historical/portrait figures. Painters with time to invest.

**Advanced combo:** For complex display figures, combine — use Method 2 underpainting, then Method 3 wash tinting over sealed skin for maximum depth.

## Airbrush Freckling / Speckling

For realistic skin on display pieces, add after skin is finished and sealed:

1. Thin mid-to-dark flesh or reddish-brown (Burnt Sienna 059, Red Orange 017, or Petroleum Brown S14) to near-watercolor consistency
2. Drop PSI to 8–12
3. Hold airbrush 3–5cm from surface
4. Close needle almost completely — tiny fractional trigger pull
5. Short bursts — atomizes into micro-dots, not lines
6. Focus on: nose, cheeks, upper shoulders, backs of hands, forearms
7. One pass → step back → assess. Extremely easy to overdo.

**Practice on card first.** The gap between not enough and too much is about 2 seconds of trigger time.

**Skip freckling entirely for:** Cartoon and anime figures — replace with smooth graphic highlight approach (bold contrast, no transitions).

## Recommended Method Decision Table

| Figure Type | Recommended Method |
|---|---|
| 28mm tabletop / speed priority | Method 1 (Basic) |
| Fair-skinned female / elegant display figure | Method 2A (Pinkle) |
| Rugged male / military / monster / weathered | Method 2B (Red Zenithal) |
| Historical / portrait / maximum realism | Method 3 (Wash Tinting) |
| Cartoon / anime | Method 1 simplified (2–3 layers max, no freckling) |
| First-time painter | Method 1 |
| Heroic / protagonist / luminous complexion | Method 2A (Pinkle) — sub-surface scattering effect reads as "lit from within" |
| Complex display, time to invest | Combine: Method 2 underpainting + Method 3 wash tinting |
